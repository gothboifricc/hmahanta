Confidence Interval vs. Confidence Level

Statistics • October 6, 2024
Scroll

Let's Clarify

If you are someone like me who was confusing confidence level with the frequency with which the sample averages fall within the confidence interval, read on because this is not the case.

Confidence Level actually means what are the chances that we are going to replicate the sample results, given a randomised sample i.e. we don't take the exact same samples again, but, we pull completely new ones from the same population. Which is our 'confidence' that we are going to get a specified result.

Textbook Definitions

Let's take a look at the textbook definition of these two terms-

1. Confidence Interval: A range of values, derived from the sample data within which the true population parameter is estimated to lie.

2. Confidence Level: A statistical measure of the percentage of test results that can be expected to be within a specified range (the confidence interval/range). Usually this level is 95% but 90% and 99% works in some cases.

Let us breakdown these definitions with an example

Consider a sample with sample size of 200

Assume, Confidence Interval = 65-75
And, Confidence Level = 95%

Here is what 95% confidence level means, if you were to take 'many' such samples of size 200 from the population and calculate the confidence interval for each sample, 𝗮𝗯𝗼𝘂𝘁 𝟵𝟱% 𝗼𝗳 𝘁𝗵𝗼𝘀𝗲 𝗶𝗻𝘁𝗲𝗿𝘃𝗮𝗹𝘀 𝘄𝗼𝘂𝗹𝗱 𝗰𝗼𝗻𝘁𝗮𝗶𝗻 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝘁𝗿𝘂𝗲 𝗮𝘃𝗲𝗿𝗮𝗴𝗲 (𝗽𝗼𝗽𝘂𝗹𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗺𝗲𝗮𝗻). It does not mean that 95% of sample means will lie within 65-75.
It’s about the reliability of the interval estimate, not the proportion of sample means within the interval.


What do you mean 'many'? Can it be 3-5 samples?

Well, not exactly, the concept of the confidence level relies on the idea of repeated sampling, not just 3-5 samples. The minimum generally requires a large number of samples, 𝘁𝘆𝗽𝗶𝗰𝗮𝗹𝗹𝘆 𝘁𝗵𝗼𝘂𝗴𝗵𝘁 𝗼𝗳 𝗮𝘀 𝟯𝟬 𝗼𝗿 𝗺𝗼𝗿𝗲 𝗮𝘀 𝗱𝗶𝗰𝘁𝗮𝘁𝗲𝗱 𝗯𝘆 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗖𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗿𝗮𝗹 𝗟𝗶𝗺𝗶𝘁 𝗧𝗵𝗲𝗼𝗿𝗲𝗺 (𝗖𝗟𝗧).

The CLT signifies how data tends to distribute around a central value with fewer occurrences as as we more further from the centre, forming a symmetric, bell-shaped curve called 𝗡𝗼𝗿𝗺𝗮𝗹 𝗗𝗶𝘀𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗯𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻.

Some interesting natural/human phenomena that follow this type of distribution are biological traits like lengths of leaves or lifespans of organisms, heights and weights of people in a population etc.
